# Product Brief: 90-Minute Stock Narrative Research

## 1. Product Concept

**90-Minute Stock Narrative Research** is a guided research workspace that helps serious investors, finance writers, and independent analysts go from cold to conversationally fluent on a stock in 90 minutes.

The product is designed for users who want to understand:

* what a company does
* why the stock matters right now
* what narratives are driving performance
* what bulls and bears actually disagree about
* what financial assumptions matter most
* how different scenarios could affect revenue, EPS, valuation multiples, and implied price ranges
* what industry trends and historical analogues help explain the setup
* what future pivots would make each scenario more or less likely

The product does not provide buy/sell recommendations or price targets. Instead, it provides **scenario-conditioned projections**: illustrative financial and valuation paths based on explicitly stated assumptions.

The core promise:

> Go from knowing nothing about a stock to being able to talk intelligently about the company, the narrative, the financial math, the scenarios, and the historical context in 90 minutes.

---

## 2. Target User / Persona

### Primary User

The initial target user is:

**Independent analysts and finance writers who need to quickly understand and explain narrative-driven stocks.**

These users include:

* finance newsletter writers
* independent equity researchers
* Seeking Alpha / Substack-style contributors
* investment bloggers
* market commentators
* small research teams
* financially sophisticated individuals who regularly discuss stocks

They need to turn scattered information into clear research narratives.

They care about being able to explain:

* what is going on
* why the stock moved
* what the market seems to believe
* what assumptions matter
* what could change the thesis
* what historical patterns are relevant

### Secondary User

The secondary user is:

**Serious retail investors who want a structured way to deeply research a stock without using an institutional terminal.**

They may not publish research, but they want to sound informed and avoid shallow narratives.

They want to be able to say:

> “The important debate is not just whether the company is exposed to AI. It is whether that exposure produces durable revenue growth, stable margins, and a valuation multiple the market is willing to sustain.”

---

## 3. Product Philosophy

The product is built around four ideas.

### 1. Users need cognitive compression, not more information

Most stock research tools provide more data, more charts, more news, and more documents.

This product helps users compress information into a usable mental model.

The goal is not to read everything. The goal is to understand the stock well enough to discuss it intelligently and know what to watch next.

### 2. Narratives only matter if they connect to math

A stock narrative becomes useful when it is translated into financial assumptions.

Bad:

> “AI demand is strong, so the stock could go up.”

Better:

> “If AI demand remains strong, revenue growth may persist. But the stock only sees major upside if margins stabilize and the market re-rates the company from a low-margin hardware supplier to a credible AI infrastructure winner.”

The product should force the bridge:

```text
Narrative
→ operating assumptions
→ revenue / EPS path
→ valuation multiple range
→ implied price band
```

### 3. Scenarios are better than predictions

The product should not say what will happen.

It should say:

> If this scenario plays out, these are the assumptions, these are the financial outputs, these are the valuation ranges, and these are the pivots that would confirm or break it.

This allows users to reason conditionally without pretending to forecast precisely.

### 4. Historical analogues are useful when tied to failure modes and valuation patterns

Historical analogues should not be shallow comparisons.

A good analogue helps answer:

* What kind of story is this?
* Where have we seen a similar story before?
* What separated winners from losers?
* What usually breaks this kind of narrative?
* What happened to growth, margins, valuation multiples, and stock performance?
* Why might the analogy be misleading?

## 4. Golden Workflow

The core workflow is a guided 90-minute research block.

The user searches a ticker and enters a structured research experience.

### The 90-Minute Research Block

```text
0–5 min: Five-Minute Orientation
5–15 min: Business Model Primer
15–25 min: Why Now?
25–40 min: Narrative Map
40–55 min: Financial Math and Market Assumptions
55–70 min: Conditional Scenario Projections
70–80 min: Industry and Ecosystem Context
80–88 min: Historical Analogues
88–90 min: Final Talk Track
```

The user should finish with:

* a clear explanation of the company
* a clear explanation of why the stock matters now
* a clear bull/bear/counter-narrative map
* a practical understanding of key financial assumptions
* scenario-conditioned price range bands
* historical analogues and failure modes
* a concise talk track they can use in conversation

### 3-Minute Magic Moment

Within three minutes, the user should see:

* what the company does
* why the stock is interesting right now
* the current dominant narrative
* the main bull/bear disagreement
* the financial assumptions that matter most
* a preview of scenario price bands

The user should immediately feel:

> “This is not another stock summary. This is a structured way to understand the story and the math.”

* What numbers matter?
* What should the user watch next?

[Why investors are paying attention now]

Current narrative:

Main debate:
[The central bull/bear disagreement]

## 11. Conditional Scenario Projections

Purpose:

> Convert narrative scenarios into explicit financial and valuation assumptions, then show implied price range bands over time.

This is the product’s most important analytical feature.

A **Conditional Scenario Projection** is not a prediction. It is an assumption-driven model that says:

> If this scenario plays out, and if the market values the company within this range of multiples, this is the implied price band over time.

### Core Formula

The model should support two primary valuation paths.

#### Revenue-Based Valuation

```text
Revenue per share × Price-to-Sales multiple = implied share price
```

#### Earnings-Based Valuation

```text
Earnings per share × Price-to-Earnings multiple = implied share price
```

The product may also support a blended valuation.

```text
Blended implied price =
(weighted revenue-based implied price) + (weighted earnings-based implied price)
```

Weights should vary by company type and scenario.

For example:

* early growth companies may be more P/S-weighted
* profitable mature companies may be more P/E-weighted
* transitional companies may use a blend
* biotech may require probability-adjusted peak-sales or cash-flow approaches
* banks, insurers, REITs, and commodities may need domain-specific models

### Scenario Projection Structure

Each scenario should include:

#### 1. Scenario Description

Plain-English narrative.

#### 2. Operating Assumptions

* revenue growth
* gross margin
* operating margin
* tax rate
* net margin
* diluted share count
* free cash flow, where relevant

#### 3. Per-Share Financial Outputs

* revenue per share
* EPS
* optionally free cash flow per share

#### 4. Valuation Assumptions

For each time period:

* P/S low
* P/S median
* P/S high
* P/E low
* P/E median
* P/E high

#### 5. Implied Price Outputs

For each time period:

* P/S implied low / median / high
* P/E implied low / median / high
* blended implied low / median / high

#### 6. Price Range Band Chart

A single chart showing:

* current price
* low band
* median path
* high band
* scenario milestones
* relevant pivot dates

#### 7. Key Sensitivities

The 2–5 variables that most affect the price band.

#### 8. Confirmation / Break Signals

Evidence that would make the scenario more or less likely.

## 12. Mathematical Model Specification

The initial projection model should be simple, transparent, and editable.

### Base Inputs

```text
Current share price
Current diluted share count
Current revenue
Current net income
Current EPS
Current revenue per share
Current gross margin
Current operating margin
Current net margin
Current P/S
Current P/E
```

### Projection Periods

Default periods:

```text
Current
+6 months
+12 months
+24 months
+36 months
```

Alternative period structures may be used for event-driven companies.

### Revenue Projection

For each period:

```text
Projected Revenue_t = Revenue_(t-1) × (1 + Revenue Growth Rate_t)
```

### Revenue Per Share

```text
Revenue Per Share_t = Projected Revenue_t / Diluted Shares_t
```

### Net Income Projection

Simple version:

```text
Net Income_t = Projected Revenue_t × Net Margin_t
```

More detailed version:

```text
Gross Profit_t = Revenue_t × Gross Margin_t

Operating Income_t = Revenue_t × Operating Margin_t

Pre-Tax Income_t = Operating Income_t - Interest Expense_t + Other Income_t

Net Income_t = Pre-Tax Income_t × (1 - Tax Rate_t)
```

### EPS Projection

```text
EPS_t = Net Income_t / Diluted Shares_t
```

### P/S Implied Price

```text
P/S Implied Price_t = Revenue Per Share_t × P/S Multiple_t
```

For each period, calculate:

```text
P/S Low Price_t
P/S Median Price_t
P/S High Price_t
```

### P/E Implied Price

```text
P/E Implied Price_t = EPS_t × P/E Multiple_t
```

For each period, calculate:

```text
P/E Low Price_t
P/E Median Price_t
P/E High Price_t
```

### Blended Implied Price

```text
Blended Low_t =
(P/S Low Price_t × P/S Weight_t) +
(P/E Low Price_t × P/E Weight_t)

Blended Median_t =
(P/S Median Price_t × P/S Weight_t) +
(P/E Median Price_t × P/E Weight_t)

Blended High_t =
(P/S High Price_t × P/S Weight_t) +
(P/E High Price_t × P/E Weight_t)
```

Where:

```text
P/S Weight_t + P/E Weight_t = 1
```

### Negative or Low Earnings Cases

If EPS is negative, near-zero, or distorted, P/E may be disabled or given low weight.

Rules:

```text
If EPS <= 0:
- do not use P/E as a primary valuation output
- use revenue, gross profit, EBITDA, book value, NAV, or domain-specific model as appropriate

If EPS is temporarily distorted:
- label P/E as low reliability
- use normalized earnings if justified and sourced
```

### Multiple Selection

The model should not invent multiples arbitrarily.

Multiples should be derived from a combination of:

* current company multiple
* company historical range
* peer group range
* narrative cluster range
* historical analogue range
* market regime context
* credibility discount or premium
* user overrides

Each multiple should include a rationale.

Example:

```text
P/E median: 18x
Rationale: assumes the market values the company as a profitable but lower-quality AI hardware supplier rather than a software-like AI platform.
```

### Scenario Probability

The initial product should avoid assigning precise probabilities by default.

It may optionally use qualitative likelihood labels:

* low confidence
* plausible
* increasingly plausible
* currently unsupported
* dependent on specific pivots

Probability-weighted price targets should not be the default.

## 13. Scenario Projection Types

Each covered stock should usually include 4–5 scenarios.

### Scenario A: Bull Narrative Validates

The optimistic thesis plays out.

Common assumptions:

* revenue growth remains strong
* margins stabilize or expand
* credibility improves
* sector narrative remains supportive
* valuation multiple expands or normalizes

### Scenario B: Good Business, Bad Stock

The business performs reasonably well, but the stock disappoints.

Common assumptions:

* revenue grows
* margins remain weak
* market applies a lower multiple
* expectations were too high
* valuation compresses

This scenario is crucial because it teaches users:

> A narrative can be directionally true while the stock still disappoints.

### Scenario C: Credibility Repair / Multiple Normalization

The key upside comes less from business acceleration and more from trust repair.

Common assumptions:

* operating results remain stable
* filings, audits, regulatory issues, or governance concerns improve
* risk discount narrows
* valuation multiple expands

### Scenario D: Bear Narrative Takes Control

The skeptical thesis becomes dominant.

Common assumptions:

* revenue slows
* margins compress
* key assumption breaks
* management credibility weakens
* valuation multiple compresses

### Scenario E: Sector / Theme De-Rating

The company may execute, but the broader theme loses market support.

Common assumptions:

* sector multiple compresses
* investor appetite fades
* macro or capex cycle turns
* theme-driven premium disappears

This is useful for stocks tied to AI, EVs, GLP-1s, cloud, commodities, renewables, crypto, or other broad narratives.

| Sector de-rating         | [summary] |                [$X–$Y] | [theme multiple]         | [sector capex]        |

### Scenario Detail View

Each scenario should show:

1. narrative description
2. operating assumption table
3. valuation multiple table
4. projected revenue / EPS table
5. implied price band chart
6. sensitivity analysis
7. confirm / break signals
8. historical analogue support
9. source notes and assumptions

### User-Editable Assumptions

Users should be able to change:

* revenue growth
* gross margin
* operating margin
* net margin
* share count
* P/S low/median/high
* P/E low/median/high
* P/S vs P/E blend weights
* time horizon

The chart should update immediately.

This is important for trust. The user should not feel forced to accept the model’s numbers.

### Assumption Rationale

Each default assumption should have a rationale:

```text
Default:
FY+1 revenue growth: 25%

Rationale:
Based on recent revenue growth, management guidance, and the scenario assumption that AI server demand remains strong but normalizes from peak growth.
```

### Sensitivity Display

The product should show which assumptions matter most.

Example:

```text
This scenario is most sensitive to:
1. gross margin
2. revenue growth
3. P/E multiple
```

### Profit Pool Analysis

The product should explain:

* who has pricing power
* who is supply constrained
* who faces competition
* who has recurring economics
* who is exposed to capex cycles
* who benefits from hype but may not capture durable economics

## 18. Final Talk Track

Purpose:

> Help the user explain the stock intelligently after the research block.

The product should provide:

### 30-Second Explanation

A concise verbal summary.

### 2-Minute Explanation

A richer conversational explanation.

### Smart Nuance

A point that makes the user sound thoughtful.

Example:

```text
The important point is not whether the theme is real. The theme can be real while the stock disappoints if the company captures less of the profit pool than investors expected, or if the market compresses the valuation multiple.
```

### Common Misconception to Avoid

A warning against simplistic takes.

Example:

```text
Avoid saying “AI demand is strong, so the stock should go up.” The better question is how much of that demand converts into durable revenue, margins, earnings, and valuation support.
```

### One-Sentence Thesis Map

```text
This is a [narrative type] story where the stock depends on [key assumptions], and the main pivots are [key pivots].
```

## 21. Trust, Safety, and Quality Rules

Because the product is investment-adjacent and includes implied price ranges, trust and safety are critical.

### No Buy/Sell/Hold Recommendations

The product should not provide:

* buy recommendations
* sell recommendations
* hold recommendations
* personalized portfolio advice
* investment suitability advice

### No Price Targets

The product should avoid language like:

* target price
* fair value
* expected price
* predicted return
* should trade at

Preferred language:

* scenario-conditioned implied range
* illustrative price band
* assumption-driven valuation range
* not a forecast
* not investment advice

### Explicit Assumptions

Every projection must show its assumptions.

The user should be able to see:

* revenue growth
* margins
* share count
* P/S multiple
* P/E multiple
* blend weights
* rationale

### Editable Assumptions

Users should be able to override model assumptions.

This reduces false authority and encourages reasoning.

### Source-Backed Claims

Major factual claims should be source-backed.

### Separate Facts, Narratives, and Models

The product should distinguish:

* fact
* interpretation
* narrative
* scenario
* projection
* historical analogue

### Label Uncertainty

The product should label:

* weak source coverage
* sparse historical analogues
* low-confidence assumptions
* highly sensitive model outputs
* distorted EPS or margins
* unusual valuation regimes

### Avoid Hindsight Leakage

Historical analogues should separate:

* period-correct evidence
* later outcomes
* modern interpretation

### Analogy Breakers Required

Every historical analogue must include why the analogy may be misleading.

## 25. Risks and Mitigations

### Risk: The projections look like price targets

Mitigation:

Use careful language: scenario-conditioned implied ranges, not predictions or targets. Always show assumptions.

### Risk: Users overtrust the model

Mitigation:

Make assumptions editable. Show sensitivity. Label uncertainty. Avoid precise probabilities.

### Risk: Bad assumptions produce misleading outputs

Mitigation:

Show assumption rationale, source evidence, peer/historical context, and user overrides.

### Risk: The financial model is too simplistic

Mitigation:

Start simple but transparent. Add domain-specific models over time.

### Risk: Historical analogues are shallow

Mitigation:

Require financial pattern, outcome, lesson, and analogy breaker for each analogue.

### Risk: Source coverage is incomplete

Mitigation:

Show source coverage and gaps. Prefer transparent incompleteness over confident synthesis.

### Risk: Product becomes too complex

Mitigation:

Keep the guided 90-minute workflow as the organizing principle. Progressive disclosure: simple summary first, deeper model only when expanded.

### Risk: Compliance concerns

Mitigation:

Avoid recommendations, personalized advice, price targets, or probability-weighted investment conclusions.
